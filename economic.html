Tariff Talk, Part One

By: Terry Roy - Published on March 14, 2025

Tariffs have always been one of America’s favorite weapons in the economic battlefield. Slap a tax on foreign goods, make them more expensive, and suddenly American-made products look a whole lot better. On paper, it sounds like a solid strategy—protect U.S. industries, save jobs, and reduce dependence on foreign markets. But in reality, tariffs are far more complicated. They are both a shield and a sword, and depending on how they’re used, they can either strengthen the economy or cut into the very people they were meant to protect.

At their best, tariffs give American businesses breathing room. When industries like steel, manufacturing, or even agriculture get hit hard by cheap foreign competition, tariffs act as a barrier, leveling the playing field. A country like China, with lower labor costs and government-backed subsidies, can flood the U.S. market with cheap products, making it nearly impossible for American companies to compete. By imposing tariffs, the U.S. forces foreign companies to raise their prices, making domestic goods more appealing. Jobs stay in America, factories keep running, and the economy gets a boost—at least in theory.

But there’s always another side to the story. Tariffs might hit foreign companies first, but they don’t absorb the cost—we do. The moment tariffs go into effect, businesses that rely on imported materials see their costs go up. A car manufacturer that depends on foreign steel now has to pay more, which means the price of that new truck you were eyeing just went up. The same thing happens with electronics, appliances, and even food. What was meant to protect American industries often ends up punishing American consumers.

Then there’s the global fallout. Tariffs aren’t just about money; they’re about power, and when the U.S. uses them aggressively, other countries don’t just sit back and take it. They retaliate. China, the European Union, and Canada have all responded to U.S. tariffs with their own, targeting American exports like soybeans, whiskey, and automotive parts. Farmers, factory workers, and small businesses—people who had nothing to do with these trade wars—end up taking the hit. Suddenly, a tool that was meant to protect jobs starts costing people their livelihoods.

Even when tariffs are used for good—like pressuring foreign governments to stop unfair trade practices—the long-term effects are messy. Take the U.S.-China trade war as an example. The goal was to hold China accountable for intellectual property theft and forced technology transfers, but along the way, American businesses lost billions due to supply chain disruptions and increased costs. The global economy doesn’t operate in a vacuum—every restriction sends ripples that impact industries in ways even policymakers can’t fully predict.

Despite the risks, tariffs aren’t going anywhere. They remain one of the few economic tools that the U.S. government can wield without waiting for Congress to act. Politicians love them because they make for strong campaign promises—"We’re putting America first!"—but rarely do they talk about the consequences. Tariffs can be necessary, even effective, in certain cases. But they are not a long-term fix for deeper economic issues.

The real challenge isn’t just foreign competition—it’s innovation, automation, and the evolving global marketplace. American industries don’t just need protection; they need to adapt. The U.S. can throw up barriers, tax imports, and fight for dominance, but at the end of the day, the most powerful economy will always be the one that out-thinks, out-creates, and out-innovates the rest. Tariffs may buy time, but they will never be a substitute for true economic strength.
